Intro to the Tool  
Hello, mysterious reader! Welcome to the dark  
halls of the TFB tool documentation. I hope  
that your stay here is as pleasant as possible,  
and, more importantly, you are excited about  
using our tools. The purpose of this  
documentation is to guide you from knowing  
nothing to being well-equipped enough to  
create and explore on your own. Though you  
might not know how to make Skylanders from  
scratch, you'll have an idea of the methods you  
can employ to start building more complex  
projects and understanding scripts that have  
already been written.

Scripting Reference  
The tools we use and the TFB approach go hand in hand, so understanding the  
philosophies behind them is where I'd like to start. Though knowing how to use  
the tool is the end goal, learning a bit of the why will help provide perspective on  
the journey.

Rapid Prototyping and Iteration  
In that case that they don't know what they want, giving designers full control  
allows them to iterate very quickly. We feel it is better to actually test out an idea  
as quickly as possible before investing too heavily in it on the design side, as it  
may not be as good as one thought. On the ﬂip side, great ideas might come from  
out of nowhere, and the best way to sell someone on an idea is to prove it. Our  
philosophy and tools encourage rapid creation and iteration, and I, personally, feel  
it is our greatest strength.  
Tools: Limited but not Limiting  
To borrow a saying from the architect of our scripting language, it is unwise for a  
tool to give them enough rope to hang themselves with. What I mean by that is  
that the simpler the tool's interfaces, the less likely a designer will accidentally  
ﬁnd the wrong way to do something. We try to keep the toolset as limited as  
possible, only exposing new features when a designer absolutely cannot do  
something he wants. For example, we exposed a portal object when we started  
working on Skylanders to provide an interface to the portal, something a designer  
had never needed to do.  
Power of Parameterization  
That said, much like with real tools, we can accomplish quite a lot by making the  
very small, limited set of tools have a lot of power and possibilities. We have about  
20 commonly-used script operations, and, of those, I'd say about 10 of them are  
the most common and useful. Though it creates an interesting learning curve, the  
idea is that when you learn about some concept/operation in the tool, it has  
applications beyond how to solve the task at hand.  
We try to keep things as consistent as possible, so the ways you interact with  
actors, particles, sounds, sprites, etc. all have some things in common, even  
though the objects may be diﬀerent. The same operation that works on actors  
works on all other objects as well - a limited tool like set value can be very  
powerful if the values you can set on any object are useful/powerful.
